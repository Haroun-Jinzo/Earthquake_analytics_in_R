5 Limitations

  1. Data Age: 1948 data may have less precise instruments than modern seismology
  2. Quality Codes: Many observations marked as “questionable” quality
  3. Single Event: Primarily one earthquake, limiting generalizability
  4. Missing Data: Some intensity values not recorded

6 Real-World Implications

6.1 For Seismology

  • Confirms basic principles of seismic wave propagation
  • Shows importance of multi-station networks
  • Demonstrates intensity-distance relationship

6.2 For Public Safety

  • Lake Tahoe region has historical seismic activity
  • Magnitude 8 events can be felt over 200+ km away
  • Multiple station monitoring is crucial for accurate measurement

7 Recommendations for Future Analysis

  1. Compare with modern data from the same region
  2. Analyze aftershock patterns more deeply
  3. Include more seismic events for broader conclusions
  4. Incorporate geological data about fault lines

9 Conclusion

ImportantMain Takeaway

This analysis of the 1948 Lake Tahoe earthquake demonstrates:

  1. A significant magnitude 8 earthquake occurred on December 28, 1948
  2. The event was recorded across a wide network of stations
  3. Seismic intensity follows expected physical principles (decreases with distance)
  4. Both California and Nevada experienced the same event with consistent magnitude
  5. Historical seismic data can provide valuable insights into regional earthquake patterns

The analysis confirms our understanding of earthquake physics and highlights the importance of the Lake Tahoe region in California-Nevada seismology.
